\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Tongji Palm-Class-Disjoint Cross-Session Results}

The primary cross-session palmprint evaluation is conducted under the development/test palm-class-disjoint protocol on the Tongji dataset. Table~\ref{tab:strict_tongji_ablation} reports the performance metrics for the complete ResNet18 model matrix (M0, M1, M2, M3, M4, M6, and M7). Values are bidirectional six seed-direction unit means and standard deviations, without pre-averaging.

\input{sections/strict_tongji_ablation_table}

The primary comparison of interest is between the baseline model M1 (CE + SupCon) and the angular-margin configuration M6 (BNNeck + ArcFace). Under the strict protocol, M6 does not yield a consistent improvement over M1. Specifically, M6 changes Rank-1 by $-1.18$ percentage points (PP), Rank-5 by $-1.00$ PP, Macro-F1 by $-1.30$ PP, EER by $+1.02$ PP (worse), TAR@FAR=$10^{-2}$ by $-2.39$ PP, and TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$ by $-2.07$ PP. These findings demonstrate that transferring normalization and angular-margin heads from face recognition to contactless palmprint recognition does not guarantee performance gains under held-out palm-class cross-session protocols.

To evaluate alternative angular-margin heads, configuration M3 (CosFace) is also evaluated. M3 achieves a mean Rank-1 of $92.63\% \pm 1.65\%$, which is very close to ArcFace (M2: $92.64\% \pm 1.09\%$) and also trails the M1 baseline ($93.39\% \pm 3.27\%$). These results further support the finding that angular-margin classifier heads do not consistently enhance held-out representation quality compared to standard contrastive feature learning (M1).

\input{sections/paired_statistics_component_ablation_table}

Table~\ref{tab:paired_statistics_component_ablation} reports paired difference statistics over the six matched seed-direction units. For the M6 $-$ M1 comparison, the mean paired delta is unfavorable to M6 for all reported metrics. The EER degradation is the most consistent paired effect, with a bootstrap interval above zero ($[+0.52, +1.52]$ PP) and an exact sign-flip value of $p = 0.03125$. For Rank-1 and TAR metrics, the bootstrap intervals cross zero, so these results should be interpreted as directional paired evidence rather than as strong formal significance claims.

\subsection{Directional and Ablation Analysis}

\input{sections/strict_tongji_ablation_by_direction_table}

Table~\ref{tab:strict_tongji_ablation_by_direction} shows that the average degradation is highly direction-dependent. In the S1$\rightarrow$S2 direction, M6 performs close to M1 in Rank-1 ($-0.14$ PP) and achieves a small strict low-FAR verification gain ($+1.21$ PP in TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$), though its EER is still higher by $+0.85$ PP. Conversely, in the S2$\rightarrow$S1 direction, M6 trails M1 more consistently, with a Rank-1 delta of $-2.22$ PP, EER increase of $+1.18$ PP, and a substantial TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$ drop of $-5.34$ PP. The bidirectional average should therefore be read as hiding directional asymmetry rather than reflecting a single uniform performance characteristic.

\subsection{Score-Distribution Diagnostics}

To inspect low-FAR behavior beyond aggregate TAR values, Table~\ref{tab:strict_tongji_score_diagnostics} summarizes genuine and impostor cosine-score distributions for the strict Tongji ablation runs. The impostor $q_{0.999}$ statistic approximates the high-impostor-score tail, while d-prime summarizes average genuine/impostor separation.

\input{sections/strict_tongji_score_diagnostics_table}

The diagnostics refine the interpretation of the ablation results. M4 (BNNeck + CE) has the highest observed mean among the tested variants by Rank-1 and TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$, but M1 has the highest d-prime, indicating the largest average separation between genuine and impostor scores. M4 instead has the lowest impostor mean among the tested variants, which is consistent with its favorable low-FAR recognition result. M6 and M7 do not improve the score-distribution diagnostics over M1; their d-prime values are lower and their score-tail behavior does not support a reliable ArcFace-based advantage under the strict protocol.

\input{sections/strict_tongji_failure_tail_table}

Table~\ref{tab:strict_tongji_failure_tail} explains why the low-FAR ranking differs from the average-separation view. In S1$\rightarrow$S2, M4 reduces the high-impostor-score tail relative to M1 and gains $+7.16$ percentage points in TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$ despite a lower genuine mean and lower d-prime. In S2$\rightarrow$S1, the same M4 comparison loses $-5.40$ percentage points at TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$, showing that the BNNeck+CE benefit is direction-dependent rather than direction-invariant. M6 shows the same asymmetry more weakly: it has only a small S1$\rightarrow$S2 low-FAR gain and a clear S2$\rightarrow$S1 degradation relative to M1. These score-tail deltas support the interpretation that ArcFace and BNNeck affect the impostor tail and score calibration differently across session order, rather than producing a uniform improvement in embedding quality.

\subsection{Palmprint-Specific Fixed-Gabor Reference Baseline}

\input{sections/palmprint_specific_baseline_table}

Table~\ref{tab:palmprint_specific_baseline} adds a palmprint-specific fixed Gabor texture reference under the same strict Tongji gallery/probe splits. This baseline is deterministic and uses no learned checkpoint, so it should be interpreted as a protocol-normalized classical texture reference. The fixed Gabor baseline reaches a competitive Rank-1 mean of $92.99\% \pm 1.86\%$, but its verification behavior is substantially weaker, with $9.61\% \pm 0.90\%$ EER and $34.38\% \pm 5.17\%$ TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$. This contrast highlights the need to report both identification and low-FAR verification metrics: a palmprint-specific texture representation can remain competitive for nearest-gallery identification while failing to match learned embeddings in the low-FAR impostor-tail regime.

\subsection{IITD Secondary Palm-Class-Disjoint Validation}

The IITD validation is a within-session evaluation. After the splits were audited and corrected to ensure gallery and probe palm-class alignment, the baseline models were rerun.

\input{sections/iitd_subject_disjoint_table}

Table~\ref{tab:iitd_subject_disjoint} reports the corrected IITD results. M6 is best interpreted as near-tied with M1 rather than clearly superior. Its mean Rank-1 is $97.95\% \pm 1.71\%$ compared with $97.83\% \pm 0.72\%$ for M1, corresponding to a minor $+0.12$ PP delta. However, M6 has slightly worse mean EER ($3.24\% \pm 1.34\%$ versus $3.05\% \pm 1.18\%$ for M1) and lower mean TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$ ($-0.72$ PP). This secondary validation on a distinct database is consistent with our main conclusion: BNNeck + ArcFace does not yield a direction-invariant improvement under palm-class-disjoint protocol constraints.
