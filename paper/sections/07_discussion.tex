\section{Discussion}

The palm-class-disjoint results change the interpretation of the BNNeck + ArcFace variant. In the original seen-identity Tongji protocol, B6 appears favorable. However, when development and test identities are made palm-class-disjoint, B6 does not outperform the B1 CE + SupCon baseline overall. This difference shows that the evaluation protocol is a first-order determinant of the empirical conclusion.

The main failure mode is not uniform across session directions. B6 has a limited gain at the strict TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$ operating point in the S1$\rightarrow$S2 direction, but it trails B1 on most other S1$\rightarrow$S2 metrics and underperforms B1 across all reported metrics in S2$\rightarrow$S1. This indicates that angular-margin supervision and BNNeck can shape useful embedding geometry in some settings, but that the effect does not translate reliably to held-out palm classes under both session directions.

The score-tail diagnostics support the same conservative conclusion. Although B5 has the best Rank-1 and TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$ among the strict ablation variants, B1 has the largest d-prime and therefore the strongest average genuine/impostor separation. This mismatch between separation diagnostics and low-FAR recognition metrics suggests that no single component dominates all operating regimes. The ArcFace-based variants do not provide a consistent score-tail advantage over the CE + SupCon baseline.

The paired uncertainty estimates reinforce this cautious interpretation. Across the six paired Tongji seed-direction units, the mean deltas for B6 relative to B1 are unfavorable on all reported metrics, but most bootstrap intervals remain wide because of the small number of paired units. Thus, the evidence is sufficient to reject a universal B6-improvement claim, while remaining too small to support a broad statistical dominance claim in the opposite direction.

Table~\ref{tab:tongji_session_quality} reports a session-level image-quality audit for Tongji segmented images. Both sessions contain 6000 images, and the average differences between session2 and session1 are small: +1.09 in mean intensity, +0.15 in intensity standard deviation, and +0.21 in Laplacian sharpness. These statistics do not prove that image quality alone causes the directional performance asymmetry. Instead, they support a more cautious interpretation: the two acquisition sessions are measurable but closely matched at the level of simple image-quality summaries, so the observed S1-to-S2 versus S2-to-S1 differences likely reflect a combination of session shift, identity sampling, and metric-learning sensitivity rather than a single obvious brightness or blur artifact.

The IITD palm-class-disjoint within-session protocol is included as secondary validation, not as cross-session evidence. It shows that B6 is competitive with B1, with very small mean deltas in Rank-1, Rank-5, EER, and TAR@FAR=$10^{-2}$, but a negative delta at TAR@FAR=$10^{-3}$. This reinforces the paper's protocol-sensitive interpretation rather than a universal improvement claim.

These results do not imply that BNNeck or ArcFace are ineffective for palmprint embeddings. They indicate that their benefit is conditioned on protocol design, identity overlap, and session direction. A fair report must therefore distinguish seen-identity evidence from held-out palm-class evidence and should avoid claims of dataset-independent dominance.

External benchmark leadership and cross-dataset robustness are not claimed. Future work should evaluate additional datasets, add verified cross-dataset protocols, and test whether the direction-specific failure observed on Tongji is due to session image characteristics, split design, or training objective mismatch.
