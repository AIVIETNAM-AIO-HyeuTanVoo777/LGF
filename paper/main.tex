\begin{abstract}
Evaluation protocols in palmprint recognition vary substantially in identity overlap, session direction, gallery construction, and low-FAR threshold policy, making component-level conclusions difficult to compare across studies. We present an audited empirical study of recognition heads and training losses for palmprint embeddings under held-out palm-class evaluation. The study covers bidirectional Tongji cross-session matching and secondary within-session validation, and compares cross-entropy, supervised contrastive learning, BNNeck, angular-margin classifiers (ArcFace, CosFace), and palmprint-oriented texture references under common splits, checkpoint-selection rules, and conservative verification metrics. The analysis shows that recognition-head gains observed under seen-identity diagnostics do not reliably transfer to held-out palm-class cross-session testing. In particular, BNNeck+ArcFace does not yield a direction-invariant improvement over a cross-entropy plus supervised-contrastive baseline, while BNNeck with standard cross-entropy exhibits direction-dependent low-FAR behavior. Score-tail diagnostics and threshold audits further show that Rank-1 identification and strict low-FAR verification can rank components differently. These findings support an evaluation-centered conclusion: reproducible palmprint studies should report audited class-disjoint protocols, bidirectional session results, and conservative empirical-FAR thresholds before making component-level improvement claims.
\end{abstract}
